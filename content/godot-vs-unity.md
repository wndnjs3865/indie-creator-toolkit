## How to choose

- **Pick [Godot](https://godotengine.org)** for indie projects, especially 2D, especially if revenue is uncertain. The MIT license + zero royalty model is dominant for solo and small-team work in 2026.
- **Pick [Unity](https://unity.com)** if you're shipping 3D commercial games at scale, mobile is your primary platform, or you specifically need the asset store ecosystem.

## The math

Unity Personal is free up to $200k/yr revenue. Past that you pay $25/user/mo for Pro. Godot is $0 at every scale, forever, no asterisks.

For most indie projects, neither tool's licensing matters in year one (you're not at $200k). The decision is about what happens *if* you cross that threshold — and about whether you trust Unity not to introduce another runtime-fee proposal in 2027.

## Verdict

| Situation | Pick |
|---|---|
| Indie 2D project, any revenue | **Godot** |
| Indie 3D project, low/medium scope | **Godot** |
| 3D commercial / AAA-aspiring | **Unity** |
| Mobile-first commercial | **Unity** |
| Care about open-source insurance | **Godot** |
| Need the massive asset store | **Unity** |
| Building portfolio for studio hiring | **Unity** (still industry standard) |
| Solo dev, revenue uncertain | **Godot** |

If you're between the two — most indie devs in 2026 should default to Godot. The threshold to switch to Unity is real (3D commercial scope, mobile pipeline depth, asset-store dependency) — but it's specific. If you don't hit one of those, Godot's economics and freedom dominate.
